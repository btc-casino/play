What is the “house edge”?

It's the statistical advantage of the casino (“house”) over the player. Obviously, in this case, less is better: lower house edge means higher payout for the player.

In a game with a 50% chance to win and a 50% chance to lose — like a coin toss — a player betting $1 could expect to have $2 (win) or 0$ (loss) at the end. But the casino always pays out slightly less.

Consider a "dice" game with a 50% chance to win, and a $1 bet. The house edge is 1%. There are two equally possible outcomes:
— the player loses $1 to the casino
or:
— the player wins, and receives a profit of 98 cents.

Suppose the player won one game and lost another. From the casino's viewpoint, during the two games, the player left just 2 cents in the casino but brought a total of $2 to the table. Divide one by the other and the result is 1⁄100, or 1% — which is the advertised house edge.

But from the player's perspective, there is a total of 98 cents to win and $1 to lose. In case of win, the casino still pays back only 98% of the profit that the player could expect in a fair game against the casino (or, the casino's fee charged on the player's profit is 2%).

It gets worse for the player if he prefers games with high probability of winning.
Consider a dice game with a 90% chance of winning, and a $100 bet won by the player:

That's right. You let the casino charge you as little as 1% or as much as 20% on your profits.

Depending on whether your casino has the edge of 0.1% or 2%.

This is why the house edge value is crucial.
